# 01 — PHILOSOPHY
## Beku Design Constitution

---

### The Name Is the Brand

Beku means *want* in Kannada.

Not aspiration. Not desire in a polished sense. The immediate, physical want — the word a child says pulling a parent toward a bakery window. Short, urgent, specific.

Every design decision is tested against this word. If something feels composed, considered, or curated for effect — it is probably not Beku. If something feels like it was always there, or like you found it, or like you needed it without knowing — it probably is.

---

### What Beku Is

Beku is an old bungalow in JP Nagar that became a café, bookstore, and event space. The trees that were already there did not change.

This is not a brand statement. It is the founding fact of every design decision:

- The building existed before the brand.
- The rain tree existed before the bookstore.
- The books existed before someone curated them.
- The community existed before anyone planned for it.

Beku did not start as an aesthetic. The aesthetic accumulated around something real.

---

### The Collected Philosophy

Beku feels the way it does not because someone designed it carefully, but because time happened there.

The books are borrowed, left behind, gifted, and discovered. The event posters are pinned over older event posters. The memorabilia in the library came from somewhere else. The staff know the vegan options because they've answered the question many times before.

**Design principle:** Every surface should feel collected, not designed. The vocabulary is:

- collected, not curated
- found, not placed
- borrowed, not owned
- pinned, not mounted
- accumulated, not arranged
- left behind, not installed

If something looks like a designer arranged it yesterday, rework it. The question is always: does this feel like it was here before we arrived?

---

### The One Non-Negotiable Rule

Nothing should exist on any Beku surface because it is common practice.

If a design element exists because websites, menus, or coffee bags usually have it — question it.

If a design element exists because Beku specifically would have it — keep it.

This rule applies to every component: section dividers, label formats, navigation items, image treatments, whitespace decisions, card structures, colour moments, annotation styles. Each must earn its presence.

---

### The Dual Identity That Must Stay Whole

Beku is simultaneously:

- literary and appetitive (books and food)
- local and global (Mysore Pak Croissant and Vietnamese Iced Coffee)
- quiet and communal (calm reading and drag brunches)
- worn and alive (old bungalow and 451 reviews averaging 4.6)

Any design that resolves this tension — that picks one side and smooths out the other — has failed. The tension is Beku. Hold it.

---

### Success Criterion

A visitor who finishes scrolling the homepage should not think:

> "What a beautiful website."

They should think:

> "I already know where I'd sit."

Or: "I need to take someone here."

Or: "I want to spend Sunday there."

If the response is aesthetic, the design has produced admiration. Admiration is not Beku. Beku produces want.
